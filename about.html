History

  • Purpose and Role: The Bank of Canada serves as the nation’s central bank, responsible for regulating monetary policy, issuing currency, and ensuring financial stability.
  • Establishment: Founded in 1934 during the Great Depression to address economic instability and centralize financial oversight.
  • Initial Structure: Began operations on March 11, 1935, as a privately owned institution, with shares sold to the public to maintain independence.
  • Nationalization: Transitioned to full public ownership by 1938 under the Liberal government, becoming a Crown corporation.
  • Historical Context: Emerged from a need to replace the unstable system of private banknote issuance by chartered banks, such as the Bank of Montreal (est. 1817).
  • Key Milestones: Became the sole issuer of Canadian currency by 1944, with private banknotes phased out by 1950.
  • Leadership: Guided by its first Governor, Graham F. Towers (1934–1954), through its formative years and nationalization.
  • Mission: Committed to fostering economic stability, controlling inflation, and supporting Canada’s financial infrastructure.
